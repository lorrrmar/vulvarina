Ignoring the physical and philosophical aspect of this, one reason why we came up with the construct of time might be the fact that our bodies have internal clocks. Chronobiology looks at our circadian rhythms - when do we sleep, when are we awake, when are our gut bacteria most active, when do our cells regenerate, when are our hormones peaking etc. To throw in some etymological knowledge - circadian comes from the two Latin words circa (around) and diem (day), describing the solar cycle of one day. In our culture, the sun has mostly been compared to male power - from Greek mythology to modern religion (weirdly, for Germans the sun is feminine and the moon masculine, but who gets them anyways). Being jet lagged is the most spread first-world-problem problem.

But in this universe there are more celestial bodies, besides the sun. One of them being the previously mentioned moon. Which coincidentally needs 28 days to go through all its phases. What other rhythm in nature takes roughly the same time? Menstruation! Women shed their uterus lining and their unused eggs every month, after going through four different hormonal phases. Follicular phase, ovulation, luteal and menstrual phase.

Notice how when I was writing about the circadian rhythm, everything sounded so scientific and accurate, while as soon as I was speaking about the moon and periods, for many of us this article started sounding unserious or a bit dreamy-vague?

Just because we have not gathered enough data about a specific subject, it does not mean it is just some esoteric nonsense. In 2017, Jeffrey Hall, Michael Rosbash, and Michael Young received the Noble Price for uncovering which gene determines the particularities of our circadian cycle. We are basically coming to terms (backed by data and solid scientific research) with the fact that our bodies are connected to nature and the universe. We need to live accordingly to our environment. And we have been living according to it in an instinctual manner.

So how comes we do not know more about the cycle half of the human population follows, in addition to the circadian one? The answer to that is that we do not direct enough of our resources to study and explore female related biology.

But if living against the circadian clock has such unhealthy and uncomfortable consequences, what are the repercussions of not living in sync with our menstrual cycle? Abdominal pain, tiredness, moodiness, PMS. Women are not broken or weak. We just know too little about our bodies and how to handle them.

Brief description of the male hormonal cycle: testosterone peaks during the morning, which means energy, confidence, impulsiveness, temperament and high sex drive. It then decreases throughout the day, with the afternoon striking a golden balance. That is a really good time for getting work done, as they will be focused and more open to teamwork. In the evening, when testosterone hits its lowest point, it is common for energy to dip with it, bringing in calmer emotions and tiredness, along with lower sex drive.

All that energy to propel awake and ready to attack your day early in the morning, a long afternoon of productivity and hyper-focus, and then tiredness starts to settle in just as you clock off from work just perfectly matches the standard work day. And it feels so natural that you are probably wondering how else could it be? Well, there are 2432534 other ways that we could structure our work lives.
